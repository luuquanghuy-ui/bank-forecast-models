# 2.1 Four-Fold Walk-Forward Cross-Validation

## Why Four-Fold?

### Problems with Single Split
- Results depend on specific random split
- May not generalize to other time periods
- Cannot assess model robustness

### Solution: Four-Fold Walk-Forward
- Expanding window: each fold uses more data
- Tests on different time periods
- More reliable estimates of model performance

---

## Fold Structure

```
Fold 1: Train 50% | Val 15% | Test 15%
Fold 2: Train 65% | Val 15% | Test 15%
Fold 3: Train 80% | Val 10% | Test 10%
Fold 4: Train 90% | Val 5%  | Test 5%
```

### Why Expanding Window?
- Later folds have more training data
- More realistic: models improve over time
- Tests robustness across different data sizes

---

## Models Evaluated

### Volatility Models (target: |log_return|)
- Naive: Predict 0
- GARCH(1,1): Standard GARCH model
- Ridge: Linear regression with L2 regularization
- RF: Random Forest (100 trees, max_depth=10)
- Hybrid: 0.5 × GARCH + 0.5 × Ridge
- NP_vol: NeuralProphet for volatility

### Price Models (target: close price)
- Naive: Predict last known price
- Ridge_price: Ridge regression for price
- RF_price: Random Forest for price
- NP_price: NeuralProphet for price

Note: GARCH is not used for price prediction (not designed for it)

## Key Findings

### 1. RF Wins Volatility Prediction
- Random Forest consistently outperforms all other models
- GARCH is solid but not best
- NeuralProphet struggles with volatility prediction

### 2. Ridge Wins Price Prediction
- Ridge regression beats Naive baseline
- RF and NP overfit on price data
- Short-term mean reversion explains Ridge success

### 3. Why RF Works for Volatility
- Volatility clustering is a strong pattern
- RF can capture non-linear relationships
- Ensemble of trees reduces overfitting

### 4. Why Not GARCH for Price
- GARCH is designed for volatility, not price
- Price follows martingale: E[S_t+1|S_t] = S_t
- Any model predicting price will have high error
